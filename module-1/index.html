01 · Cryogenics fundamentals

What is cryogenics?

Cryogenics is the branch of physics that studies the effect and production of very low temperatures: the process of generating temperatures as close as possible to absolute zero.

  • Absolute zero (0 K) equals −273.15 °C: the theoretical limit where molecular motion ceases.
  • The US National Bureau of Standards considers temperatures below −150 °C (−240 °F) to be cryogenic.
  • Industrial gases liquefied in this range include nitrogen, oxygen, hydrogen, helium, and natural gas (LNG).

LNG is handled at −162 °C: well below the cryogenic threshold.

Solid

01 · Cryogenics fundamentals

Key concepts in cryogenic behavior

1

Saturation

State in a closed vessel where liquid and gas coexist in equilibrium at the same temperature.

2

Subcooling

Increase in pressure in the gas phase above that of the saturated vapor.

3

Vaporization

Liquid → gas when energy (heat) is added to the saturated liquid.

4

Condensation

Gas → liquid when energy (heat) is removed from the gas.

5

Cavitation

Formation of vapor bubbles within a liquid.

6

Stratification

Warmer (less dense) liquid migrates upward while colder liquid remains at the bottom of the tank.

01 · Cryogenics fundamentals

Cryogenic liquid behavior

  • Liquid growth: as heat and pressure are added, molecules gain energy and occupy more space; the liquid expands inside the tank.
  • Pressure–temperature relationship: the higher the pressure, the higher the liquid saturation temperature.
  • Density–temperature relationship: the higher the temperature, the lower the liquid density.
  • Cryogenic cylinders are filled to safe levels to leave expansion room (vapor space) and prevent relief valves from opening.
25%
volume growth: 100 L → 125 L when going from 0 to 150 psig (same weight: 81 kg)
31%
flash loss: LN₂ tank at 120 psig vented down to 0 psig
These same principles apply to stored and transported LNG.
01 · Cryogenics fundamentals

Heat transfer

Energy transported from a hot object to a cold one, through three mechanisms:

Conduction
Occurs through direct contact between objects or within a single object.
Examples: piping, supports; stainless steel (medium value), aluminum/copper (high value)
Convection
Heat movement driven by density gradients between internal cold and external heat.
Examples: vacuum insulation, pressure-building vaporizer coil
Radiation
Energy transfer as a function of surface emissivity.
Examples: multi-layer super-insulation, tanks painted white

02 · What is LNG?

What is LNG?

Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) is natural gas cooled to a liquid state, at approximately −162 °C, to make it easier to store and transport over long distances where pipelines do not exist.

  • Composed mainly of methane (85–95 %).
  • Cryogenic liquid: density ≈ 450 kg/m³ (less than half that of water).
  • Colorless, odorless, non-toxic and non-corrosive.
  • Volume shrinks about 600× upon liquefaction (1 m³ LNG = 600 Sm³ of natural gas).

Key safety considerations

  • LNG vapor is lighter than air above −110 °C and disperses; below that temperature it is heavier and can accumulate at ground level.
  • Evaporated LNG can displace oxygen and create an asphyxiation risk in confined spaces.
  • Rollover: stratified layers of different densities in a storage tank can trigger a sudden vapor release if they invert.
  • LNG is not flammable in its liquid state: it only burns in gaseous phase, mixed with air within its flammability range.
04 · LNG safety

Protective equipment and first aid

Personal protective equipment

  • Hard hat and face shield · safety glasses (under the shield).
  • Long cryogenic gloves.
  • Aluminized / flame-resistant apron, or Nomex suit with no open pockets.
  • Long-sleeve shirt, pants, work boots · personal CH₄ detector.

First aid

  • Skin: remove contaminated clothing, rinse with cold or lukewarm water (never hot); medical attention for blisters/frostbite.
  • Asphyxiation: fresh air, artificial respiration or O₂; immediate medical attention.
  • Eyes: rinse cold/lukewarm, protect from light, medical attention.
  • Ingestion: do not induce vomiting; rinse mouth, water or milk; immediate medical attention.
04 · LNG safety

Fire suppression

  • NEVER use water or foam on LNG.
  • Use dry chemical extinguishers: Purple K recommended — 5× the extinguishing power of CO₂.

05 · Comparison

Costs and infrastructure

  • LNG — high infrastructure cost (liquefaction & regasification plants, cryogenic tanks); competitive cost per unit of energy in large volumes and long distances.
  • LPG — moderate infrastructure (simple pressurized tanks); widely available, with a higher cost per unit of energy than natural gas in most markets.
  • CNG — moderate compression infrastructure; generally low cost per unit of energy, ideal for urban fleets with return to base.
  • Diesel — minimal, consolidated global infrastructure; cost per unit of energy subject to oil price volatility.
05 · Comparison

Emissions and sustainability

  • LNG / CNG: lower carbon footprint, virtually no sulfur or particulate emissions.
  • LPG: intermediate — cleaner than diesel, more carbon than natural gas.
  • Diesel: highest NOx and particulate emissions; requires additional after-treatment.

06 · Advantages, challenges, and conclusions

Advantages and challenges of LNG

Advantages

  • Lower carbon footprint than diesel and other petroleum-derived liquids.
  • High energy density — efficient long-distance transport.
  • Enables international gas trade without pipelines.
  • Clean combustion: no sulfur or significant particulate matter.

Challenges

  • High investment — liquefaction, regasification, specialized vessels.
  • Complex cold chain — requires −162 °C at all times.
  • Risk of oversupply and price volatility in the global market.
  • Methane emissions (leaks) along the chain if not properly managed.
06 · Conclusions

In summary

  • Cryogenics makes it possible to liquefy natural gas at −162 °C, shrinking its volume ~600× and allowing it to be shipped by sea to any destination.
  • Understanding pressure, temperature, density and the phenomena of saturation, vaporization and stratification is key to operating LNG safely.
  • Compared to LPG, CNG and Diesel, LNG combines high energy density with lower emissions, though it requires greater investment in cryogenic infrastructure and strict safety protocols.
  • The optimal fuel choice depends on distance, volume to be transported, and infrastructure available at the destination.

Take-home · Recap

Key messages — Module 001

  • 1 · LNG is liquid by extreme temperature (−162 °C), not pressure; CNG shrinks volume by pressure.
  • 2 · Liquefaction = pretreat → cascade cooling → −162 °C → atmospheric storage; volume ÷ 600.
  • 3 · Tendrara: 16.67 MMSCFD → ~261 t/d LNG at −165.7 °C; small-scale MR plant, off-grid by truck.
  • 4 · Vapour burns only at 5–15 % in air; cold vapour hugs the ground below ≈ −110 °C.
  • 5 · Hazards: cryogenic burns · embrittlement · vapour cloud · RPT · rollover · asphyxiation.
  • 6 · Plant gas is NOT odorized — trust instruments & detectors, never smell; never water on an LNG pool.
